## What are Israel Bonds?

Israel Bonds are direct loans to the Israeli government. Unlike most bonds, they are not traded on secondary markets and cannot be sold before maturity. This means pension funds that purchase them are locked into the investment — facing greater liquidity risk than comparable alternatives.

## What's happening in Arkansas?

Three Arkansas state agencies have invested or authorized significant funds for Israel Bonds purchases:

- **State Treasury** holds approximately **$55 million** in Israel Bonds
- **ATRS** (Arkansas Teacher Retirement System) authorized up to **$50 million** in June 2025
- **APERS** (Arkansas Public Employees' Retirement System) authorized **$25–50 million** in May 2025

These authorizations came after all three major credit rating agencies downgraded Israel's credit rating — and after an internal Treasury memo in late 2024 recommended against new purchases.

## The fiduciary problem

Arkansas law requires that pension investments be based solely on "pecuniary factors" — financial merit. Several red flags have emerged:

- **ATRS Board Chair Danny Knight voted no**, warning the process departed from normal investment practice
- **No independent credit analysis** was performed before the authorizations
- Israel Bonds sales representatives met with all three agencies in April 2025; within weeks, new purchases and authorizations followed

## The symmetry argument

Arkansas's anti-BDS law (Act 710) establishes that investment decisions must be based on financial merit, not political motivation. This standard should apply equally in both directions: if it's wrong to sell investments for political reasons, it's equally wrong to buy them for political reasons.

The question isn't political — it's financial. Were these investment decisions made through proper due diligence, or were they driven by outside pressure?
