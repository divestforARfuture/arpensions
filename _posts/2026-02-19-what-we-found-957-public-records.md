Over the past year, Divest for AR Future filed Freedom of Information Act requests with four Arkansas state agencies: the State Treasury, the Arkansas Teacher Retirement System (ATRS), the Arkansas Public Employees' Retirement System (APERS), and the Auditor of State. We received and analyzed **hundreds of public records** — board packets, internal memos, correspondence, meeting minutes, and financial documents.

Here is what those records tell us.

## The money

Arkansas's exposure to Israel Bonds is approximately **$155 million**:

- The **State Treasury** holds $55 million, including $20 million in new purchases since May 2025. Of the existing holdings, $25 million matures in 2026, $10 million in 2028, and $20 million in 2030.
- **ATRS** authorized up to $50 million in Israel Bonds on June 2, 2025 (Resolution 2025-22).
- **APERS** authorized $25–50 million in May 2025 through an Investment Subcommittee resolution.

To be clear: these amounts are a small fraction of each fund's total portfolio — less than 0.5% of APERS's roughly $11 billion fund and less than 0.25% of ATRS's roughly $22 billion fund. But the significance of what we found isn't about the dollar amounts. It's about the process.

## The internal warning that was overridden

In late 2024, a senior investment manager in the State Treasurer's office assessed the financial risks of Israel Bonds and recommended caution. His memo noted that "both S&P Global Ratings and Moody's downgraded Israel's credit ratings due to heightened security risks and weakened economic prospects" and that "the outlook remains negative." His recommendation: hold existing positions and allow maturing bonds to roll off — in other words, no new purchases.

By spring 2025, that recommendation had been overridden. The Treasury purchased $20 million in new bonds, and both pension boards authorized up to $100 million more. What changed between the late-2024 memo and the spring 2025 buying spree was not the financial outlook — it was the political pressure.

## The sales pitch

In April 2025, Israel Bonds sales representatives met with the directors of ATRS and APERS, the State Treasurer, and the State Auditor. These meetings were arranged by State Auditor Dennis Milligan, who serves as an ex officio trustee on both pension boards.

Within weeks of those meetings, the Treasury made new purchases, and both pension boards voted to authorize large Israel Bonds allocations. A thank-you note from an Israel Bonds executive to Milligan praised his "support and passion for Israel Bonds" and called him "critical to our success and Israel's future."

## The dissent

Not everyone went along. ATRS Board Chair Danny Knight was the sole "no" vote on the June 2025 authorization. He warned that the Board was "going outside of the scope of the way we usually do things" — selecting a specific bond investment at a trustee's suggestion, rather than relying on the professional investment managers who normally make those recommendations.

Knight's objection went to the heart of fiduciary process: the standard practice at ATRS is for investment decisions to originate from professional staff and consultants, not from board members or political appointees. The Israel Bonds authorization reversed that process.

## What we didn't find

Sometimes what's missing from the record is as important as what's there.

In hundreds of documents across four state agencies, we found **zero independent credit analyses** of Israel Bonds prepared before the authorizations. No side-by-side comparison of risk, return, and liquidity with comparable alternatives. No written recommendation from ATRS's investment consultants (Aon Hewitt) endorsing the purchase.

This is unusual. For a $50 million authorization in a relatively illiquid, single-country sovereign bond with a declining credit rating, you would expect — at minimum — a documented financial analysis showing why this specific investment was the best available option for pension fund members. We found no evidence that such an analysis was performed.

## The political framing

The public record also includes statements from state officials that frame these investments in explicitly political — not financial — terms:

- Governor Sanders: "Arkansas puts its money where its mouth is and is investing millions in Israeli bonds!"
- State Treasurer Larry Walther, press release (Oct. 2023): "Investing in Israel Bonds is a testament to our longstanding belief in Israel's resiliency and the value it brings to the global economy."
- Deputy Auditor Brady, at the APERS meeting: referenced the U.S. Ambassador to Israel as "my and Amy's former boss" and called Israel "the United States' most trusted and dependable ally in a volatile region."

These statements matter because Arkansas law requires pension investments to be based solely on "pecuniary factors" — financial merit. When the people championing an investment describe it in political terms, it raises the question of whether the legal standard was met.

## What it means

We're not asking anyone to take our word for it. The records are public. The law is clear. The questions are straightforward:

1. Were these investments evaluated solely on pecuniary factors — risk, return, and liquidity?
2. Was an independent credit analysis performed and documented before the authorizations?
3. Why were the internal staff recommendations against new purchases overridden?
4. How does the process — where a state official arranged sales meetings and championed a specific investment — comply with Arkansas's fiduciary standards?

These are the questions we'll keep asking. And we'll keep using the tools Arkansas law gives us — FOIA requests, public comment, and advocacy — to make sure they get answered.
